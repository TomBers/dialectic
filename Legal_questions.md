Legal questions

Yes — there **can be intellectual-property issues**, especially with contemporary copyrighted sources like an `Economist` article. You’re right to seek professional advice. I’m not a lawyer, but here are the main practical issues to think about.

## 1. The graph itself may be a derivative work

When you transform an article into a structured argument graph, you are not merely storing facts. You may be creating a **derivative work** if the graph substantially reproduces the article’s protected expression, structure, selection, arrangement, or wording.

A very high-level graph that says:

- “The article argues AI tycoons resemble historical capitalist magnates”
- “It compares AI leaders to Ford, Rockefeller, Carnegie”
- “It identifies three commonalities: eccentricity, risk, and state regulation”

is more likely to be treated as commentary, summary, or factual analysis.

But a graph that closely tracks the article paragraph by paragraph, uses many quotations, preserves its framing, and can substitute for reading the original article is more legally risky.

## 2. Facts are not protected, but expression is

Copyright generally does **not** protect facts, ideas, or historical claims. For example, these are factual or idea-level elements:

- ChatGPT has many users.
- AI leaders include Altman, Amodei, Hassabis, Musk, and Zuckerberg.
- Ford popularised the Model T.
- Standard Oil was broken up in 1911.
- The article compares AI magnates to earlier tycoons.

But copyright **does** protect the article’s original expression:

- distinctive phrasing
- narrative structure
- vivid comparisons
- selection and arrangement of examples
- particular sentences and paragraphs
- headline/subheadline language

So a graph should ideally extract **ideas and argument structure**, not reproduce the article’s prose.

## 3. Direct quotes are the biggest obvious risk

Using short quotations for criticism, commentary, or scholarship may be permissible in some contexts, depending on jurisdiction and circumstances. But quoting extensively from a paywalled/current article is risky, especially if your graph is public.

For the Economist article, the graph included several direct blockquotes. That is something I would treat cautiously.

A safer approach for copyrighted journalism would be:

- use **minimal direct quotes**
- quote only what is necessary to identify or comment on the argument
- avoid long blockquotes
- avoid reproducing the “best” or most distinctive prose
- include citation/source attribution
- link to the original article rather than replacing it

## 4. Fair use / fair dealing may or may not apply

Depending on where you are, doctrines like **fair use** in the US or **fair dealing** in the UK/Commonwealth may help, but they are fact-specific.

Common questions include:

- Is the use transformative?
- Is it for commentary, criticism, research, education, or commercial use?
- How much of the original is used?
- Is the “heart” of the work copied?
- Could your graph substitute for the original article?
- Does it affect the market for the original?
- Is the source paywalled or licensed?

A graph that is analytical, critical, and heavily paraphrased is more defensible than one that reproduces lots of quoted article text.

## 5. Paywalled journalism is especially sensitive

The Economist’s business depends on subscriptions/licensing. If your public graph allows users to get the article’s substance without visiting/subscribing, they could object even if you think the graph is transformative.

Even if the legal merits are debatable, there is practical risk:

- takedown request
- licensing demand
- platform complaint
- reputational issue
- terms-of-service issue if the article was accessed under subscriber terms

## 6. Public-domain works are much safer

The Russell, Orwell, Dostoevsky, and some older works may be public domain depending on jurisdiction and edition/translation. But even there:

- translations can have separate copyright
- annotations or modern editions may be protected
- public-domain status varies by country

For example, Dostoevsky’s original text is public domain, but a specific modern translation may not be. The Constance Garnett translation is generally public domain in many contexts, but you’d still want to verify for your jurisdiction.

## 7. Contemporary books are also sensitive

For something like Marletto’s `The Science of Can and Can’t`, direct quotations should be very limited. A paraphrased conceptual graph is much safer than one with lots of extracted text.

For modern copyrighted books/articles, I’d recommend treating the graph as:

- notes
- commentary
- conceptual summary
- citation/link companion

not as a replacement for the source.

## 8. Attribution helps but does not solve copyright

Attribution is good practice and may be required ethically or under some licences, but it does **not** by itself make copying lawful.

Saying “source: The Economist” does not give permission to reproduce protected expression.

## Practical safer policy for your app

For copyrighted contemporary sources, I’d suggest these rules:

1. **No long direct quotes.**
2. **Use mostly paraphrase.**
3. **Limit quotations to short phrases necessary for commentary.**
4. **Always include source title, author/publication, date if known, and link.**
5. **Avoid making the graph a substitute for the original.**
6. **Consider keeping copyrighted-source graphs private unless you have permission/licence.**
7. **Add a “source / citation” metadata field or node.**
8. **For public display, include only argument abstractions and minimal snippets.**
9. **If a rights-holder asks for removal, have a takedown process.**

## For The Economist graph specifically

The potential issues are:

- It is a current copyrighted article.
- The graph follows the article’s structure closely.
- It includes several direct quotes.
- If public, it might serve as a substitute summary for some readers.

A safer revision would:

- remove most direct quotes
- keep only 1–3 short identifying quotes
- paraphrase the rest
- add a source citation/link
- possibly mark it private or unpublished unless you have permission

## Bottom line

Yes, there are plausible IP concerns with turning an Economist article into a public graph, especially if it includes direct quotations and closely tracks the article. A paraphrased, analytical graph is safer; a quote-heavy, article-substitute graph is riskier.

For public-domain texts, you have much more room. For modern journalism/books, I’d use minimal quotes, strong attribution, and consider permission or keeping the graph private.
